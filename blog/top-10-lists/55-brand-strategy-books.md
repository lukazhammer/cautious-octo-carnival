# 10 Brand Strategy Books Every Entrepreneur Should Read

These 10 books provide frameworks, strategies, and mental models for building powerful brands. Read them to understand positioning, differentiation, and strategic brand building.

Listed in recommended reading order for entrepreneurs building brands.

## 1. "The Brand Gap" by Marty Neumeier

**Why read it:**
Perfect introduction to brand strategy. Short, visual, accessible. Explains what brand actually is (not logo) and how strategy and design must work together.

**Key concepts:**
- Brand is gut feeling, not what you say it is
- The brand gap between strategy and creativity
- Five disciplines of brand building
- Charismatic brands vs. commodity brands

**Best for:**
Entrepreneurs just starting to think strategically about brand. First book to read.

**Key takeaway:**
"A brand is a person's gut feeling about a product, service, or organization." You don't control brand—you influence customer perception through strategic consistency.

**Practical application:**
- Understand brand as perception, not identity
- Align strategy and design
- Create differentiation, not just distinction
- Build for long-term brand equity

**Who should read:** Everyone building a brand. Start here.

## 2. "Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind" by Al Ries and Jack Trout

**Why read it:**
The foundational positioning book. Published 1981, still relevant. Explains why you can't be everything to everyone and how to own a position in customer's mind.

**Key concepts:**
- Positioning is about customer perception, not product features
- Own a word or concept in prospect's mind
- First in category wins (or create new category)
- Repositioning competition as important as positioning yourself

**Best for:**
Anyone struggling with differentiation or trying to compete in crowded markets.

**Key takeaway:**
"Positioning is not what you do to a product. Positioning is what you do to the mind of the prospect."

**Practical application:**
- Choose specific positioning (narrow beats broad)
- Own one clear concept
- Position against competitors strategically
- Create new category if can't win existing

**Who should read:** Founders defining positioning strategy.

## 3. "Building a StoryBrand" by Donald Miller

**Why read it:**
Clarifies brand messaging using story framework. Makes complex positioning simple through 7-part StoryBrand framework.

**Key concepts:**
- Customer is hero, you're guide
- Clarify their problem
- Provide plan
- Call to action
- Seven-part StoryBrand framework

**Best for:**
Entrepreneurs struggling to communicate value clearly. Confused messaging.

**Key takeaway:**
Stop making your brand the hero. Customer is hero. Your brand is guide helping them succeed.

**Practical application:**
- Reframe messaging: customer-centric, not company-centric
- Clarify what problem you solve
- Simple, clear message
- Effective website and marketing copy

**Who should read:** Anyone writing website copy, sales materials, marketing messages.

**[Develop brand storytelling →](/blog/strategy-framework/brand-storytelling/)**

## 4. "Obviously Awesome" by April Dunford

**Why read it:**
Modern positioning framework specifically for tech and B2B. Practical, actionable steps for defining and testing positioning.

**Key concepts:**
- Position

 is context for understanding value
- Five components of positioning
- Positioning canvas framework
- Market category choices
- Competitive alternatives frame value

**Best for:**
SaaS founders, B2B companies, product marketers defining positioning.

**Key takeaway:**
"Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best at something that a defined market cares a lot about."

**Practical application:**
- Use positioning canvas systematically
- Choose market category strategically
- Frame competitive alternatives
- Test positioning with customers

**Who should read:** B2B and SaaS founders doing positioning work.

## 5. "How Brands Grow" by Byron Sharp

**Why read it:**
Evidence-based brand science. Challenges conventional marketing wisdom with data. Controversial but important perspectives.

**Key concepts:**
- Mental and physical availability drive growth
- Brand loyalty is weak (most customers buy multiple brands)
- Reach light buyers, not just loyal customers
- Distinctive brand assets matter more than positioning claims

**Best for:**
Marketers and founders wanting data-driven brand perspective beyond traditional positioning theory.

**Key takeaway:**
Brands grow by reaching more buyers (mental availability) and being easy to buy (physical availability), not by creating cult-like loyalty.

**Practical application:**
- Build distinctive brand assets (colors, sounds, symbols)
- Reach broad audience, not just narrow niche
- Make brand easy to buy
- Focus on availability, not just preference

**Who should read:** Marketers balancing Sharp's research with positioning theory. Read after positioning books.

**Note:** Some principles conflict with narrow positioning. Take insights, apply appropriately to your context.

## 6. "The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing" by Al Ries and Jack Trout

**Why read it:**
Timeless strategic principles. Short, punchy, memorable. Each "law" is positioning principle.

**Key concepts:**
- Law of Leadership: Better to be first than better
- Law of Category: If can't be first, create new category
- Law of the Mind: Perception beats reality
- Law of Focus: Own one word
- 18 other strategic laws

**Best for:**
Quick strategic frameworks and mental models for brand decisions.

**Key takeaway:**
Strategic principles that guide brand and marketing decisions. Violate them at your peril.

**Practical application:**
- Create new category if can't lead existing
- Focus positioning on single concept
- Narrow focus beats broad positioning
- Use principles as decision framework

**Who should read:** Entrepreneurs wanting strategic mental models.

## 7. "Zag: The Number One Strategy of High-Performance Brands" by Marty Neumeier

**Why read it:**
When everyone zigs, zag. About radical differentiation through opposite positioning.

**Key concepts:**
- Radical differentiation beats incremental improvement
- 17-step brand strategy process
- Find whitespace in crowded markets
- Opposite positioning strategy

**Best for:**
Brands in crowded markets needing differentiation breakthrough.

**Key takeaway:**
"When everybody zigs, zag." True differentiation comes from doing opposite of competition, not slightly better version.

**Practical application:**
- Study what competitors do
- Do opposite strategically
- Find overlooked market segments
- Create blue ocean through differentiation

**Who should read:** Founders in competitive markets seeking differentiation.

## 8. "Differentiate or Die" by Jack Trout

**Why read it:**
Modern follow-up to Positioning. Focuses specifically on differentiation in hyper-competitive markets.

**Key concepts:**
- Differentiation is survival requirement
- Types of differentiation that work
- Why "quality" and "service" don't differentiate
- How to find and own differentiation

**Best for:**
Businesses stuck in "me too" positioning needing to break out.

**Key takeaway:**
Without differentiation, only alternative is price competition. Differentiation is business strategy, not just marketing.

**Practical application:**
- Identify authentic differentiation
- Avoid commodity positioning
- Make differentiation specific and provable
- Build entire business around differentiation

**Who should read:** Anyone competing on price or stuck in commodity market.

## 9. "Blue Ocean Strategy" by W. Chan Kim and Renée Mauborgne

**Why read it:**
About creating uncontested market space (blue ocean) instead of competing in bloody red oceans. Strategic canvas framework for finding whitespace.

**Key concepts:**
- Red ocean (bloody competition) vs. blue ocean (uncontested)
- Value innovation: differentiation + low cost
- Strategy canvas for visualizing positioning
- Four actions framework (eliminate, reduce, raise, create)

**Best for:**
Founders wanting to create new categories or redefine markets.

**Key takeaway:**
Don't compete in existing markets (red ocean). Create new market space (blue ocean) where competition is irrelevant.

**Practical application:**
- Map competitive factors on strategy canvas
- Eliminate factors industry takes for granted
- Raise factors above industry standard
- Create factors industry never offered
- Find blue ocean positioning

**Who should read:** Entrepreneurs creating new categories or disrupting industries.

## 10. "Start With Why" by Simon Sinek

**Why read it:**
About purpose-driven branding. Why mission and values create powerful differentiation and loyalty.

**Key concepts:**
- Golden Circle: Why, How, What
- People don't buy what you do, buy why you do it
- Purpose-driven brands inspire action
- Biology of decision-making

**Best for:**
Founders building mission-driven brands or struggling with commoditization.

**Key takeaway:**
"People don't buy what you do; they buy why you do it." Purpose differentiates when products commoditize.

**Practical application:**
- Articulate why your company exists beyond profit
- Lead messaging with purpose and mission
- Attract customers who share values
- Build culture around purpose

**Who should read:** Mission-driven founders and purpose-based brands.

## How to Read These Books Strategically

**Foundation (read first):**
1. The Brand Gap - Understand what brand is
2. Positioning - Learn positioning fundamentals
3. Building a StoryBrand - Clarify messaging

**Positioning depth:**
4. Obviously Awesome - Modern positioning framework
5. The 22 Immutable Laws - Strategic principles
6. Differentiate or Die - Competitive differentiation

**Advanced strategy:**
7. Zag - Radical differentiation
8. Blue Ocean Strategy - Category creation
9. How Brands Grow - Evidence-based perspective
10. Start With Why - Purpose-driven branding

**Reading plan:**
- Month 1-2: Books 1-3 (foundation)
- Month 3-4: Books 4-6 (positioning depth)
- Month 5-6: Books 7-10 (advanced strategy)

## Applying What You Learn

**After each book:**
1. **Summarize key concepts** (write 1-page summary)
2. **Identify 3 actions** to apply to your brand
3. **Implement immediately** (don't just read, apply)
4. **Review quarterly** (revisit notes, refine strategy)

**Common mistake:**
Reading without application. Books provide frameworks—you must do the work applying them to your brand.

**Better approach:**
- Read one book
- Apply frameworks to your business
- See results
- Read next book
- Layer new concepts

## Books to Skip (Overrated or Outdated)

**Not recommended:**
- Generic "personal branding" books (usually fluff)
- Outdated pre-internet marketing books
- Books focused solely on visual identity
- "Secret formula" branding books
- Books without frameworks or evidence

**Red flags:**
- No clear framework
- Anecdotes without principles
- Written by someone who hasn't built successful brand
- Published pre-2000 without updates
